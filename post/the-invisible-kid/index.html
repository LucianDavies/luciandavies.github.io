The invisible kid

June 9, 2020

The story unfolds when I was 14 years old. I was in my second year in all boys high school. At that time is was already evident that I struggled with school. The whole idea of structured learning grated my soul. Anyhow it was 2 term and parents teacher consultations for all form 2’s pupils scheduled. The process goes something like this. After school day. Class then ending at 1pm all the parents would pitch up and meet the teachers and get a 1 on 1 update or insights into their children’s performance. I had 10 subjects all in all and this story involved a French teacher called Mx Tiribabi and a history teacher called Mx Mawarire. Mx being because I don’t remember if they were married or not and it doesn’t matter.

So my mother rolls up to school and didn’t take any time finding her place in the parents teacher consultation dance. I didn’t waste my time waiting for her cause soon after she was going to go back to work so I headed home. These things are a bit nerve racking because the parents tend to swallow the teachers perception of you and if this perception was not pretty things could get ugly very quickly.

Mother arrives home after work a bit tired but with enough energy to sit me down. She unfolds a piece of paper with all her notes and the first thing she said is you are doing well but you need to participate more in French. I was a bit taken back and asked why and she said she sat down in front of Mx Tiribabi’s place and after a brief intro, Mx Tiribabi went into a long monologue about how you were very noisy in class and that you didn’t do you homework and worse that most times you did not even attend classes. My heart was beating faster. This was not the tone I expected for such a report even though clearly some of this was fabricated. Mother said the Mx Tiribabi went on and on until Mx Mawarire sitting next to Mx Tiribabi leaned in and asked if she was talking about me cause it was wildly different the boy she knows and somewhat out of character. Mother then said Mx Tiribabi then paused for a second then with a blank face asked:

wait, so who is your child?

With that final nail in the coffin it became clear that after 2 years of learning French the French teacher didn’t know who I was. At that point I understood why the tone of voice. I had survived that consultation. If it wasn’t for Mx Mawarire things would have been bad. On the other hand ever since that consultation, the French teacher paid special attention to me for the remaining 2 years. She would highlight our homework books in red as if fencing some imaginary villain then scream:

Khatai and your stupid friend Murimirwa, get out!!

And with that we spent most days outside, having been kicked out of French for not doing our conjugations right or failing to count with the right pronunciation and mostly talking, free to roam the big school while trying to evade ruthless prefects and head of departments patrolling the hallways.
